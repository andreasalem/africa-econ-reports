Ghana Mines Stage 1: Pipeline + Baseline Results

Project C · Progress Report — 2026-05-08

Project C
Ghana Mines
Stage 1
Full pipeline build and first training run for Galamsey mine detection in Ghana. U-Net on S2 NDVI bands achieves val_AUC = 0.861, beating Christensen et al. (2025) benchmark of 0.75. Hansen label contamination identified as the precision ceiling; six workstreams planned.
Author

Andrea Salem

Published

May 8, 2026

1 Summary

Built a complete detection pipeline for illegal artisanal gold mines (Galamsey) in Ghana and ran the first baseline model. The U-Net (unet_tiny, 4 S2 NDVI bands) achieved val_AUC = 0.861, beating the Christensen et al. (2025) benchmark of 0.75 by a wide margin. Training converged in 25 epochs (7.5 hours on Quest A100) with early stopping.

Recall is very high (0.988) but precision is low (0.437). Root cause: Hansen deforestation labels conflate mining with cocoa farming and logging — the cocoa belt and gold belt overlap in southwest Ghana. Six workstreams are planned to address label quality before the next training iteration.

2 Motivation

Ghana faces a severe illegal artisanal gold mining (Galamsey) crisis. Galamsey operations cause deforestation, mercury water contamination, and destruction of agricultural land. The problem is concentrated in the gold-bearing Birimian greenstone belts of southwest Ghana (Ashanti, Western, Eastern regions).

Goal: Reliably detect Galamsey mine sites from Sentinel-2 satellite imagery, then estimate their causal impact on local outcomes (health, water quality, economic activity).

Why this is tractable: Galamsey mines are spectrally distinctive — bare red soil and turbid water ponds carved into dense tropical forest create an extreme NDVI drop (~0.8 forest \(\rightarrow\) ~0.2 mine). This is a stronger signal than the Tanzania mines that Christensen et al. (2025) detected at AUC 0.75.

Key references:

  • Christensen et al. (2025) NBER — Mine detection across Africa, AUC 0.75 on Tanzania, RCF method (GitHub unavailable)
  • Arezki et al. (2025) Oxford — AlphaEarth + CNN mine detection, presence only
  • Snapir et al. (2017) RSE — Mining deforestation in Ghana via Landsat NDVI time series

3.4 Hansen filtering \(\rightarrow\) mining candidate patches

Applied spatial and morphological filters to separate mining-related deforestation from agricultural clearing:

Filter Positive pixels Patches
Raw forest loss (2015–2023, $$30% canopy) 11,261,686 —
+ Gold belt restriction (Ashanti, Sefwi, Kibi) 8,118,056 —
+ Patch size filter (0.1–10 ha) 5,596,902 601,017

Filter rationale:

  • Gold belt restriction: Galamsey occurs in Birimian greenstone belts. Loss outside these zones is overwhelmingly agricultural.
  • Patch size 0.1–10 ha: Mine clearings are small and irregular. Agricultural clearings tend to be >10 ha and more regular.
  • Not yet applied: River proximity filter (alluvial gold clusters within 1 km of waterways).

Output: data/ghana/labels/gfw_mining_loss.shp (601,017 polygons) + gfw_mining_loss.tif (binary raster).

4.1 Train/val split

Initial contiguous split (chunks 0–93 train, 94–116 val) placed all val chunks in northern Ghana where there are no mines — val had zero positive pixels. Fixed by interleaving: every 5th chunk goes to val, ensuring both splits sample from the gold belt. Val set contains 9 chunks with positive mine labels, spread across the full geographic range.

4.2 Key design decisions

Decision Rationale
Dry season composite (Nov–Feb) Minimal clouds; mine scars most visible

6 Interpretation

6.1 What this result means

The baseline AUC of 0.861 is strong for a first pass — the model is clearly learning to distinguish mine-affected areas from background. The high recall (0.988) means it catches nearly every mine. The low precision (0.437) means roughly half of flagged pixels are false positives.

6.2 Why precision is low (and expected to be)

The core issue is label contamination. The training labels are Hansen GFC patches filtered to the gold belt (0.1–10 ha clearings). But in Ghana, the cocoa belt and gold belt overlap almost perfectly — both follow the Birimian greenstone formation. Cocoa farm clearings in Ashanti/Western regions are the same size range and geography as Galamsey sites. The model has learned “deforestation in western Ghana” rather than “mine sites specifically.”

This is not a model failure — it is a label quality ceiling. Improving precision requires either (a) better labels or (b) mine-specific spectral features that separate bare-soil mines from agricultural clearings.

6.3 Train AUC vs val AUC gap

Train AUC (0.683) is substantially lower than val AUC (0.861). This inversion (opposite of the usual overfitting pattern) is an artifact of the patch sampling: with neg_ratio=1, training patches are balanced (~50/50), while val patches reflect the natural class distribution (17.75% positive). The model’s confident negative predictions yield higher AUC on the sparser val set.

7 Known Issues

  1. pos_weight override: The runtime pos_weight calculation sampled 50 batches from the start of the TFRecord, found zero positives (unlucky draw), and defaulted to 1.0 instead of the configured 30. Likely slowed early convergence. Fix: shuffle TFRecords or bypass runtime calculation when config specifies pos_weight.

  2. No inference-style validation: The pipeline’s post-training inference validation was skipped (“No val_chunks found in config”). Reported metrics are from Keras model.evaluate(), not sliding-window inference over full chunks. Spatial prediction maps require inference-style evaluation.

  3. 79 GPS sites not yet tested: The known Galamsey coordinates have not been validated against model predictions. This is the independent ground truth — Hansen-based val_AUC is partially circular.

8 Planned Improvements: Six Workstreams

The baseline exposes a clear path: the model architecture works and the spectral signal is strong, but the labels are contaminated. Six workstreams address this.

8.1 Phase 1 — Start now (parallel, independent)

8.1.1 W1: Spectral label filtering

Problem: 601k Hansen patches include cocoa, logging, and agriculture — not just mines.

Solution: Post-filter Hansen patches by mine-specific spectral signatures at each patch location:

  • NDVI < 0.3 (bare soil, not vegetated farmland)
  • BSI > threshold (Bare Soil Index: exposed laterite/clay)
  • NDWI > threshold (turbid mine ponds)

Expected: reduce 601k patches to ~50–100k with much higher mine purity. Directly improves precision.

Script: src/preprocessing/11_spectral_label_filter.py

8.1.2 W3: Timestamp mines from Hansen lossyear

Problem: Need mine opening dates for causal identification (event study, diff-in-diff).

Solution: Overlay filtered mining patches onto Ghana_Hansen_lossyear_2001_2023.tif. For each patch, extract modal year \(\rightarrow\) mine opening date at annual resolution. Output: GeoDataFrame with geometry, area_ha, opening_year, centroid_lat, centroid_lon.

Not ML — raster overlay on two small files (~30 MB). Runs locally in minutes. Mine opening = treatment event for causal estimation.

Script: src/preprocessing/12_timestamp_mines_hansen.py

8.1.3 W6: External label upgrade

Problem: Hansen is a noisy proxy. Better labels exist but require access.

8.2 Phase 2 — After W1 + baseline results

8.2.1 W2: Mine-specific spectral indices

Replace generic S2 NDVI bands with mine-targeted indices: BSI (bare soil), NDWI (turbid ponds), FMF (ferrous minerals / laterite), plus standard NDVI. Config task: minesGH-Ghana-MineIdx.

8.2.2 W4: Multi-temporal stacking

Stack 2018 + 2023 S2 composites (8 channels) so the model sees the forest \(\rightarrow\) mine transition directly. Requires downloading 2018 S2 for Ghana. Config task: minesGH-Ghana-Temporal.

8.2.3 W5: SAR fusion

Add Sentinel-1 VV backscatter as an extra channel. Flooded mine pits show distinctive low backscatter, providing cloud-independent signal. Config task: minesGH-Ghana-SAR.
