Ghana Mines Stage 1: Baseline Training

Project C · Progress Report — 2026-05-08

Project C
Ghana Mines
Stage 1
U-Net on S2 NDVI bands achieves val_AUC = 0.861 for Galamsey mine detection in Ghana, beating Christensen et al. (2025) benchmark of 0.75. Recall is 0.988 but precision is 0.437 — label contamination from cocoa farming is the ceiling. Spectral filtering reduces 601k Hansen patches to 4,309 mine-like candidates.
Author

Andrea Salem

Published

May 8, 2026

1 Context

This report covers the first training run for Project C — detecting illegal artisanal gold mines (Galamsey) in Ghana from Sentinel-2 imagery. For pipeline details (S2 download, Hansen filtering, GPS validation sites), see the pipeline report. For the Alpha Earth experiment plan, see the AE-16 report.

The question: Can a U-Net trained on Hansen deforestation labels detect Galamsey mines, and can we beat Christensen et al. (2025) AUC of 0.75 on Tanzania?

NDVI bands chosen based on Rwanda Stage 2b (NDVI r=0.750 vs NIR r=0.432) and mine-specific spectral contrast (forest NDVI ~0.8 drops to ~0.2 at mine scars; Snapir et al. 2017).

Headline: AUC 0.861 beats the Christensen benchmark by 0.11 on the first attempt. The extreme spectral contrast of Galamsey sites (bare laterite + turbid water in dense tropical forest) makes Ghana an easier target than Tanzania’s semi-arid mines.

3.3 Training Progression

Best weights at epoch 13 (val_AUC = 0.8614). ReduceLROnPlateau fired at epochs 11, 16, and 21. Early stopping at epoch 25.

4 The Precision Problem

Recall is near-perfect (0.988) — the model catches almost every mine. But precision is only 0.437 — roughly half of flagged pixels are false positives. This is a label quality ceiling, not a model failure.

Root cause: Ghana’s cocoa belt and gold belt overlap. Both follow the Birimian greenstone formation. The Hansen training labels mark all forest loss in the gold belt as “mining,” but cocoa farm clearings are the same size (0.1–10 ha) and geography as Galamsey sites. The model learned “deforestation in southwest Ghana,” not “mine sites specifically.”

4.1 Spectral Label Filtering (W1)

To quantify the contamination, we filtered the 601k Hansen patches by mine-specific spectral signatures at each patch centroid (NDVI < 0.4, BSI > 0.0):

Step Patches
Hansen filtered (gold belt, 0.1–10 ha) 601,017
With S2 coverage (38/70 tiles local) 57,165
Passed spectral filter (mine-like) 4,309 (7.5%)

92.5% of sampled patches were spectrally inconsistent with mining — they had high NDVI (vegetated) or low BSI (no exposed soil). These are predominantly cocoa farms and logging clearings.

Implication: Retraining with the 4,309 filtered patches should substantially improve precision — the training signal will be purer, and the model will learn to distinguish bare-soil mine scars from vegetated farm clearings.

5 Mine Timestamps (W3)

With the 4,309 spectrally-filtered patches in hand, we extracted the opening year of each mine from the Hansen lossyear raster (12_timestamp_mines_hansen.py). For each connected patch, the modal lossyear value gives the year of dominant forest clearance.

Year Patches Area (ha) Notes
2015 380 310 First year in filtered range
2016 616 532
2017 487 456
2018 407 416
2019 271 277 Dip — Operation Vanguard crackdown?
2020 556 604 Rebound — crackdown lapsed
2021 439 556
2022 730 1,040 Peak year — galamsey surge
2023 423 514
Total 4,309 4,705

Key finding: The 2019 dip (271 patches, lowest year) coincides with Ghana’s “Operation Vanguard” military crackdown on galamsey. The sharp 2020 rebound (2\(\times\) increase) followed by the 2022 surge (peak at 730 patches, 1,040 ha) suggests the crackdown was temporary. This temporal pattern provides free causal identification for impact estimation: mine opening date serves as a treatment event for diff-in-diff / event study designs without requiring any manual date annotation.

Outputs: data/ghana/labels/mine_timestamps.gpkg (4,309 features with geometry, opening_year, area_ha) and mine_timestamps.csv.

6 Known Issues

  1. pos_weight override: Runtime calculation sampled zero positives from TFRecord start, defaulted to 1.0 instead of configured 30. Likely slowed early convergence.

  2. No spatial inference validation yet. Reported metrics are from model.evaluate(), not sliding-window inference over full chunks. Scripts ready (03_predict_ghana.py, 04_validate_ghana_mines.py).

  3. W1 coverage gap: Only 38/70 S2 tiles available locally — 543k of 601k centroids had no coverage. Running W1 on Quest (all 70 tiles preprocessed) will produce a more complete filtered set.

8 Next Steps

  1. Re-rasterize W1-filtered labels — align 4,309 filtered patches to S2 imagery grid via 08_make_labels_ghana.py, producing Ghana_Mines_binary_filtered.tif for retraining.
  2. Retrain with filtered labels — expect precision improvement from 0.44 to ~0.60+ as cocoa/logging contamination is removed.
  3. Run inference (03_predict_ghana.py --all) and validate against 79 GPS sites (04_validate_ghana_mines.py) — the independent ground truth that avoids Hansen circularity.
  4. W6: External labels — 8 sources identified (Snapir polygons, Sebastian GPRL, Ghana Minerals Commission, OSM, GLAD alerts, Planet NICFI, Delve/artisanalGold). All need human action (emails, downloads). Long lead time.
  5. AE-16 experiment — most ready Phase 2 entry: 314 GB on Drive, PCA script written. Tests whether Alpha Earth reverses its Rwanda failure for mine detection.
