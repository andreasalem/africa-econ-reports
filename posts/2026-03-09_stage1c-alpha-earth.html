Stage 1c: Alpha Earth Experiments

1 Context

This report documents Stage 1c — a systematic comparison of five spectral configurations for building detection. The motivation comes from Stage 2’s error analysis, which showed that false positives and true positives are spectrally indistinguishable in the 3-band NIR/Blue/SWIR2 feature space, and that NDVI is the primary missing discriminant (Stage 2 report).

Stage 1c tests whether adding more spectral information — from a single NDVI band up to 64-dimensional Google Alpha Earth embeddings — improves building detection. All experiments use the same training data (Rwanda 2022, Open Buildings labels), spatial split, loss function, and optimizer settings validated in Stage 1b.

For background on the base pipeline and training setup, see Stage 1 and Stage 1b.

2.1 Controlled Variables

All experiments share:

  • Labels: Google Open Buildings v3 rasterized at 10m
  • Spatial split: Rows 0–2 (south/central) = train, row 3 (north) = validation
  • Loss: Focal Tversky (alpha=0.7, beta=0.3)
  • LR: 5e-6 with ReduceLROnPlateau (validated in Stage 1b)
  • Patience: 20 epochs
  • Negative ratio: 1:1 (balanced sampling)
  • Hardware: Northwestern Quest HPC (A100 GPU, 48 GB)

2.2 Google Alpha Earth Embeddings

Experiments 3 and 4 use Alpha Earth (GOOGLE/SATELLITE_EMBEDDING/V1/ANNUAL), a foundation model that encodes Sentinel-2 imagery into 64-dimensional learned embeddings at 10m resolution. These embeddings are pre-trained on global satellite imagery and are designed to capture land-use semantics that raw spectral bands may miss.

  • Resolution: 10m (matches S2)
  • Bands: 64 (learned, not interpretable as wavelengths)
  • License: CC-BY 4.0
  • Download: src/download/04_download_alpha_earth.py
  • Stacking: src/preprocessing/06_stack_s2_ae.py produces 76-band TIFs (12 S2 + 64 AE)

2.3 Dual-Branch Architecture

Experiment 4 uses unet_dual_branch, a modified U-Net that processes S2 and AE inputs through separate encoder branches before fusing at the bottleneck. This prevents the 64 AE bands from overwhelming the 12 S2 bands in a single encoder.

S2 (12 bands) → Branch A (3 conv blocks) ─┐
                                           ├── Concatenate → Shared decoder → Output
AE (64 bands) → Branch B (3 conv blocks) ─┘

Architecture defined in vendor/mlgis-ponds/src/mlgis_helpers/model.py (unet_dual_branch).

3.1 Key Observations

1. More bands ≠ better AUC. The 3-band NIR baseline achieves the highest AUC (0.976). Adding NDVI (4 bands) drops AUC to 0.941. Using all 10 S2 bands drops further to 0.938. Alpha Earth embeddings (64 bands) achieve the lowest AUC at 0.921.

2. F1 is remarkably stable. All experiments achieve F1 between 0.966 and 0.976 — a spread of only 0.01. This confirms the Open Buildings label ceiling diagnosed in Stage 1b: the labels themselves have ~0.97–0.98 accuracy, and no model can reliably exceed its training labels.

3. AE threshold is a red flag. The AE-only model requires a threshold of 0.05 to achieve its F1 — meaning it produces nearly flat probability maps where building and non-building pixels are barely separated. Compare the NIR baseline’s threshold of 0.90, which indicates well-calibrated, confident predictions. A threshold of 0.05 means the model is fundamentally uncertain about what is and isn’t a building.

4. S2+AE dual-branch partially recovers. Adding S2 bands back to AE (experiment 4) raises AUC from 0.921 to 0.931 and shifts the threshold from 0.05 to 0.20. The S2 branch provides the spectral grounding that AE alone lacks for this specific task.

4 Interpretation

4.1 Why Don’t More Bands Help at Pixel Level?

The result is counterintuitive — more spectral information should help, not hurt. Three factors explain it:

1. The NIR baseline already exploits the best discriminators. NIR, Blue, and SWIR2 were chosen because they provide the strongest built-up vs. vegetation contrast. Additional bands (Red Edge, Green, Red) add redundant information about the same spectral gradient while introducing noise. The model has to learn to ignore irrelevant bands, which is harder with limited training data.

2. Alpha Earth embeddings are not optimized for building detection. AE embeddings encode general land-use semantics (crop type, water bodies, forest structure). Building detection — distinguishing rooftops from bare soil at 10m — may not be well-represented in embeddings trained for broad-scale land classification. The embeddings likely encode what type of land a pixel is, not whether a specific structure is present.

3. The label ceiling binds all models equally. With Open Buildings labels at ~0.97 accuracy, no model can reliably exceed F1 ≈ 0.97 regardless of input quality. The AUC differences reflect confidence calibration (how well-separated building vs. non-building probability distributions are), not classification accuracy. All models hit the same ceiling; the simpler model gets there with better calibration.

4.2 The Real Test Is District Level

Stage 2 showed that pixel AUC alone is a poor predictor of economic value. The NIR baseline achieved AUC=0.976 but only r=0.38 at district level. The error analysis revealed that false positives — detecting built-up areas around buildings that Open Buildings doesn’t label — actually inflate pixel counts in some districts more than others.

If NDVI reduces false positives in vegetated districts (as the spectral analysis predicts), it could improve district-level correlation even with lower pixel AUC. Pixel AUC and district correlation can move in opposite directions. This is why Stage 2b (next report) is the definitive evaluation.
