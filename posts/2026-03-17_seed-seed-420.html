Seed Seed 420: Hyperparameter Robustness via Multi-Seed Validation

Project A · Progress Report — 2026-03-17

Project A
GDP Mapping
Methodology
Our Stage 2b finding (NDVI r=0.750) is based on a single random seed. Sebastian Sardon shows that seed variance ≈ hyperparameter variance above LR=1e-5 — most apparent model improvements are luck. This report describes the methodology and will be updated with results once 30 Quest GPU runs complete (3 tasks × 10 seeds).
Author

Andrea Salem

Published

March 17, 2026

1 Motivation

Stage 2b established a striking result: the NDVI model achieves district-level Pearson r = 0.750, a 75% improvement over the NIR baseline (r = 0.432). This would be a clean finding — except both runs used the same random seed (42).

Sebastian Sardon (Kellogg) raised a methodological concern on 2026-03-16: seed variance often dominates hyperparameter variance in neural network training. The figure below (produced by Sebastian for the mines detection pipeline) shows a learning rate sweep from 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻³, each point replicated across 10 seeds. The key observation: above LR = 10⁻⁵, within-LR variation (seeds) is roughly as large as between-LR variation. The “optimal” LR found by a single-seed sweep could easily be reversed by a different seed.

The implication is direct: a pipeline that tests hyperparameter A vs. B with seed=42 only, and reports that A wins, may simply have gotten lucky. This is the central critique of AI benchmarking raised in Karpathy’s autoresearch project (https://github.com/karpathy/autoresearch).

Note

The practical rule: If two configurations differ by less than ~2× the within-configuration seed standard deviation, the result is not reliably distinguishable.

2 What “Seed Seed 420” Means

The method, named by Sebastian:

  • Run each experiment configuration with 10 seeds: 42, 84, 126, 168, 210, 252, 294, 336, 378, 420 (multiples of 42)
  • Seed 42 is the conventional default; 420 = “seed seed 420” (10× the convention)
  • For each seed, train the full model and compute the district-level Pearson r on the held-out Rwanda census
  • Report mean ± std across seeds as the robust estimate
  • Declare a winner only if the mean distributions are non-overlapping

3 Experimental Setup

We apply this to the three surviving experiments from Stage 2b:

Task Bands Original r (2023) Seed=42 only?
buildingsNIR-Rwanda2022-1b 3 (NIR/Blue/SWIR2) 0.432 ✓
buildingsNDVI-Rwanda2022 4 (+NDVI) 0.750 ✓
buildingsALL-Rwanda2022 10 (all S2) 0.727 ✓

AE-only and S2+AE are not included — their negative district r (r ≈ -0.27) is sufficiently catastrophic that seed variation cannot rescue them.

Total compute: 3 tasks × 10 seeds = 30 GPU runs on Quest A100.

Each run takes ~2–4 hours (same as original training), so the full sweep is ~60–120 GPU-hours.

4 Infrastructure

Three new components were built for this experiment:

4.1 1. scripts/quest/train_multi_seed.sbatch

SLURM array job (--array=0-9). Each array index maps to a seed: SEEDS=(42 84 126 168 210 252 294 336 378 420). Output goes to out/mlgis_output/<task>/seed_<seed>/ to avoid collisions.

# Submit for one task:
sbatch --export=ALL,TASK=buildingsNDVI-Rwanda2022 \
    scripts/quest/train_multi_seed.sbatch

4.2 2. scripts/quest/run_stage2_seed_sweep.sbatch

Loops over 3 tasks × 10 seeds. For each, runs inference (01_predict_rwanda.py --seed <seed>) and district aggregation (02_aggregate_districts.py --seed <seed>). Skips completed runs. Chain after training:

sbatch --dependency=afterok:<array_job_id> \
    scripts/quest/run_stage2_seed_sweep.sbatch

4.3 3. src/diagnostics/seed_variance.py

Collects district r CSVs from all seed runs, produces violin plots and a summary CSV.

python src/diagnostics/seed_variance.py --year 2023

Output: out/diagnostics/seed_variance/seed_violin_2023.png and seed_r_2023.csv.

5 Hypotheses and Interpretation Guide

Three possible outcomes, with implications:

5.1 H1: NDVI robustly wins (distributions non-overlapping)

NDVI mean r >> NIR mean r with std bars that don’t overlap. Interpretation: the NDVI improvement is real. Proceed to Stage 3 (regression head with Peru data) with NDVI as the confirmed architecture. This is the hoped-for outcome.

5.2 H2: NDVI wins on average but distributions overlap

NDVI mean r > NIR mean r, but the difference is within ~1 standard deviation. Interpretation: NDVI tends to be better, but the advantage is fragile. More careful architecture search or larger training data needed before claiming r=0.75.

5.3 H3: Distributions are indistinguishable

Seed variance is so large that NIR, NDVI, and S2-all cannot be ranked reliably. Interpretation: the model is too small / training data too limited for band configuration to matter. Would suggest we need a qualitatively different approach (larger architecture, more training countries, or a fundamentally different label strategy).

7 Connection to Autoresearch

Karpathy’s autoresearch (https://github.com/karpathy/autoresearch) automates ML experiments with LLM agents. One known failure mode: LLM agents run each configuration once, take the winner, and iterate — potentially chasing noise. Sebastian’s seed sweep methodology is a principled fix: treat seed variance as the baseline noise floor and only accept improvements that exceed it.

For economics journals, seed robustness is equivalent to requiring statistical significance. A finding that holds only on seed=42 is not publishable. This sweep is our equivalent of a robustness check.
